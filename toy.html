📘 About the Boundary-Loss Surrogate Model

▼

In silicon pixel detectors like MALTA2, small pixels are grouped together (e.g. into clusters of size \(N_x \times N_y\)) to form logic units. The choice of grid geometry \((N_x, N_y)\) introduces a trade-off:

  • Larger grid widths: Reduce the coincidence of digital merges across unit boundaries, lowering boundary noise.
  • Physical encoding constraints: Large grid widths lead to longer lines, introducing routing cuts or "survival cliffs" due to signals arriving too late or suffering pile-up.

The paper models this boundary-loss surrogate \(\Phi(N_x, N_y)\) as:

$$ \Phi(N_x, N_y) = \frac{L_x \, p_x}{N_x} + \frac{L_y \, p_y}{N_y} $$

where \(p_x, p_y\) represent directional digital merging coincidence probabilities, and \(L_x, L_y\) model the physical survival rates. The parameter \(\rho = p_y/p_x\) is the physics anisotropy. Under standard isotropic conditions, \(\rho = 1.0\).

When evaluating a fixed pixel grouping area \(A = N_x \times N_y\), we obtain the constant-area aspect ratio profile by studying the normalized loss \(\hat{\Phi}\) as a function of the aspect ratio on a logarithmic scale: \(Y = \log_2(N_y / N_x)\).

The continuous optimal aspect ratio is dictated solely by the total system anisotropy parameter \(\chi\):

$$ \chi = \frac{L_x / L_y}{\rho} \implies \log_2\left(\frac{N_y^*}{N_x^*}\right) = -\log_2(\chi) $$

If \(\chi = 1.0\), the optimum aspect ratio is \(Y = 0\), which represents a square layout (\(N_x = N_y\)). When \(\chi < 1.0\) (e.g. survivable-x regime), the ideal layout shifts towards taller grid geometries (\(N_y > N_x\), positive \(Y\)), which yields substantial savings in overall digital boundary loss.
